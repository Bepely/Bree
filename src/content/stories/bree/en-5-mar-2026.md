14:50. The code is a mess, the admin panel is a disaster, and I’m about to jump ship to my day job. Perfect. 

![](/illustrations/bree/2026-03-05/57d7c701-3646-4a99-9fea-c3dc3da3e160.webp)

I’ve been running like a *belka v kolese*—a squirrel in a wheel—for a month now. It’s time to hit pause. The admin panel is the current culprit; right now, it’s just a viewing window. I can look, maybe delete, but editing and categorizing are non-existent. It’s *bespredel*—total structural chaos. I need to tear it apart and rebuild the management flow, but that’s a problem for the version of me that isn’t currently drowning under the weight of my actual day job. 

![](/illustrations/bree/2026-03-05/0979a7e4-b709-48fd-9931-4430970b31d1.webp)

Later in the day, the engine hums in the back of my mind. The pipeline is the real bottleneck. Right now, there’s no clear sense of context transferring from one record to the next—it’s just a stream of narrative noise. I’ve been sketching out a fix: separate Markdown files for every entry, using technical markers to force order. H2 headers for every section, clean placeholders for every image. If I can enforce this structure, the final output will finally feel like a coherent collection instead of a pile of digital scraps. It’s tedious, but necessary. Until the scaffolding is solid, everything else is just building on sand.

![](/illustrations/bree/2026-03-05/8df15825-82b5-4939-be02-e30b228215c3.webp)

I find myself in a restaurant, a temporary sanctuary from the monitors. I’m staring out the window, watching the street, the amber light catching the glass. My brain is still chewing on the project—the mechanics, the endless moving parts—but the noise is starting to fade. I realize I’ve been pushing against a wall for weeks, convinced that if I just sprinted harder, the wall would turn into a door. It won’t. It’s just a wall. The realization is cooling, like the air in here. 

![](/illustrations/bree/2026-03-05/080693da-99b9-479a-a184-7a35fe02f299.webp)

I inhaled my food before I could even think to document it. Just a clean plate and the quiet satisfaction of a meal finished without a screen in front of me. 

![](/illustrations/bree/2026-03-05/e375c210-69cd-4846-90b4-cd77d2044fbe.webp)

Then there’s the token issue. The "early bird" dilemma keeps circling back. Logic suggests a wrapper for OpenRouter, keeping the barrier to entry low while I maintain control of the limits. Maybe pull up an API keys status so people can see exactly how much gas they have left in the tank. Keep it clean. Keep it simple. Don’t over-engineer the bridge before the road is even paved.

![](/illustrations/bree/2026-03-05/901a4ab2-2f74-44be-987e-34d1ed74e953.webp)

It’s 23:56. The apartment is quiet, but the air is thick with the residual static of a day spent in loops. My eyes feel heavy, that specific, sandpaper-dry exhaustion that comes with staring at too many lines of logic. 

I’m done. The martyr complex can wait until tomorrow. Realizing that you’re tired isn't a failure—it’s a design choice. A system that works until 4:00 AM every night is a system that crashes. I’m choosing sustainability. I’m closing the laptop, walking away from the cables and the half-finished tasks, and letting tomorrow handle the rest. 

![](/illustrations/bree/2026-03-05/016e2358-5a29-4c19-a70f-3faa7b185f0e.webp)

The desk is a mess, but the room is still. A genuine peace sign to the camera, a nod to the fact that I’m actually shutting it down. No rendering, no late-night repo pushes. Just sleep. 

![](/illustrations/bree/2026-03-05/1e9dd95c-50ff-40d8-ab0c-7f1591d85979.webp)
